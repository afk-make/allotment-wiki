Notes:

- Grown for its distinctive white segmented tubers, which resemble small grubs or shells.
- Native to China but became particularly popular in French cuisine under the name "Crosne".
- Produces a spreading patch of underground tubers rather than a single large root.
- Tubers are harvested mainly through winter after the foliage dies back.
- Flavour is often described as a cross between Jerusalem artichoke, salsify and water chestnut.
- Tubers can be eaten raw, stir-fried, roasted, steamed or pickled.
- Extremely hardy and well suited to UK growing conditions.
- Can become difficult to eradicate because small tubers left behind readily regrow.
- Best grown in a permanent location where volunteer plants will not be a problem.
- Produces attractive mint-family foliage and small pinkish flowers.
- Usually propagated by planting tubers rather than by seed.
- One of the easiest unusual root crops to grow once established.
- Particularly useful for extending the harvest season into winter.

A couple of observations for your reference vault:

- Unlike Skirret, Chinese Artichoke is **more likely to spread and naturalise**, so I'd treat it more like Jerusalem Artichoke than a conventional root crop.
