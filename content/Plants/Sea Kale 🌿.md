Notes:

- Native British coastal perennial vegetable.
- One of the most highly regarded perennial vegetables for flavour.
- Traditionally grown for blanched spring shoots, which are often compared to asparagus.
- Young leaves can be cooked and eaten similarly to kale.
- Flower shoots and unopened flower buds can be prepared like broccoli.
- Produces attractive blue-green waxy foliage throughout the growing season.
- Forms large clouds of fragrant white flowers that are highly attractive to pollinators.
- Extremely long-lived once established and can remain productive for many years.
- Particularly well suited to perennial vegetable beds and edible landscaping.
- Requires very little maintenance once established.
- Naturally adapted to poor, coastal and sandy soils.
- One of the best edible ornamental plants available to UK gardeners.
- Generally considered superior to Good King Henry for flavour.

Growing Habit:

```
Permanent crown        ↓Spring shoots        ↓Leaves        ↓Flower shoots        ↓Repeat every year
```

Harvest Windows:

```
Mar-Apr   Blanched shootsApr-May   Young leavesMay-Jun   Flower shoots & buds
```

Spread Risk:

- Very low.
- Forms a stable crown similar to asparagus or rhubarb.
- Does not spread through runners or invasive roots.
- Can remain in the same location for many years.

Container Growing:

- Possible but not ideal.
- Recommended minimum container size: **40 litres**.
- Performs best in open ground with a permanent position.
- Similar to asparagus in that it rewards long-term establishment.

Garden vs Allotment:

- Excellent in either location.
- Attractive enough for ornamental borders.
- Productive enough for a dedicated perennial vegetable bed.
- One of the rare crops that combines:
    - Excellent flavour
    - Ornamental value
    - Long lifespan
    - Low maintenance
