Notes:

- Traditional European perennial vegetable cultivated for centuries before spinach became widely available.
- Produces edible spring shoots, leaves and flower buds.
- Young spring shoots can be harvested and cooked similarly to asparagus.
- Mature leaves are commonly used as a spinach substitute.
- Flower buds can be harvested and cooked in a similar manner to broccoli.
- Extremely hardy and well suited to UK conditions.
- One of the classic plants found in perennial vegetable gardens.
- Forms a stable crown rather than spreading through runners or tubers.
- Generally remains where planted and is considered well-behaved in permanent beds.
- May occasionally self-seed if flowers are allowed to mature, but seedlings are easy to remove.
- Can remain productive for many years with minimal maintenance.
- Particularly useful for providing fresh greens in early spring.
- More valued for reliability and longevity than for exceptional yields.
- Often described as an acquired taste compared with modern spinach varieties.

Growing Habit:

```
Plant once↓Harvest shoots in spring↓Harvest leaves through summer↓Repeat for years
```

Container Growing:

- Possible but usually unnecessary.
- Recommended container size: **20–30 litres**.
- Performs well in the ground and rarely becomes invasive.
- Long-lived plants will eventually require division if kept in containers.

Permanent Bed Suitability:

- Excellent choice for a perennial vegetable bed.
- Similar behaviour to Sorrel, Welsh Onions and Skirret.
- Much less likely to spread than Chinese Artichokes or Jerusalem Artichokes.
- Can comfortably remain in the same position for many years.
