Notes:

- Traditional Andean tuber crop grown for colourful edible tubers.
- Usually sold in the UK as mixed colours rather than named varieties.
- Produces red, pink, orange, yellow and cream tubers depending on the selection.
- Extremely easy to grow and generally unaffected by potato blight.
- Forms attractive low-growing mounds that help suppress weeds.
- Tubers begin forming only as day length shortens in autumn.
- One of the latest crops harvested on the allotment, often remaining in the ground until November or December.
- Can continue bulking up until the first hard frosts.
- Tubers become sweeter if left in sunlight for a few days after harvest.
- Can be eaten raw, roasted, mashed, stir-fried or added to stews.
- Stores reasonably well but not as long as maincrop potatoes.
- Tubers left in the ground often regrow the following spring, so volunteers are common.
- Excellent choice for gardeners looking for something unusual and low-maintenance.
- Particularly useful because it provides a harvest when most summer crops have finished.

One small thing I'd consider for your note: because mixed-colour oca often produces noticeably different tubers, you might eventually add observations in the body such as:

```
Red tubers: sweetest Yellow tubers: highest yield Pink tubers: best keeper
```

That sort of personal observation can end up being more valuable than variety data, since many mixed packs contain several unnamed clones.

Oca is propagated vegetatively.

That means:

```
Good traits✓ preserved & Bad traits✓ preserved
```

So if a particular plant is weak or diseased, don't save those tubers.

Only keep stock from your best performers.
